Understanding Portfolio Construction

The core engine of a robo-advisory system is its portfolio construction algorithm. These algorithms translate a user's risk profile into a practical asset mix with measurable return and volatility targets.

1. Equal Weighting (1/N)

The Equal Weighting strategy assigns the same capital allocation to each asset regardless of historical return, volatility, or correlation.

  • Strengths: Highly intuitive, easy to implement, and requires no complex forecasting.
  • Weaknesses: It ignores risk differences across assets and can create an equity-dominated risk profile.

2. Risk Parity

Unlike equal weighting, Risk Parity allocates risk rather than capital, aiming for balanced volatility contribution across asset classes.

  • Strengths: Delivers stronger diversification across different macro environments.
  • Weaknesses: Often depends on leverage because more capital is allocated to lower-risk assets.

The Impact of Real-World Constraints

  • Transaction Costs: Frequent trading erodes returns and requires cost-aware optimization.
  • Rebalancing Frequency: Rebalancing must maintain target risk without creating excessive fees.
  • Asset Availability: Retail portfolios may rely on liquid ETFs as practical proxies for less accessible assets.

Our Models

In order to objectively evaluate the effectiveness of this algorithm, we have conducted a comparative analysis of two different asset allocation methods: Equal Weighting 1/N Model as a benchmark, and the other is Risk-Based Allocation Model developed by us. This comparison aims to reveal the fundamental differences between simplified heuristic methods and personalized, risk-based strategies in terms of Expected Annual Returns and Annual Volatility.

1.The formula of Model A (Equal Weighting 1/N Model) and Model B (Risk-Based Allocation Model).

Our Model aims to calculate the Expected Annual Returns and Annual Volatility through the formula that involves the real American interest rate of cash and bonds and the annual return rates of S&P 500 based on the historical database.

Basic Formula

Portfolio Return = (WEquities × REquities) + (WBonds × RBonds) + (WCash × RCash)

Portfolio Volatility = (WEquities × VEquities) + (WBonds × VBonds) + (WCash × VCash)

  • W: Asset weight in the portfolio. In Model A (1/N), W = 0.33 for each asset class.
  • V: Asset volatility used in portfolio risk estimation.
  • R: Asset return in the current year.

3. Comparative Analysis of Model A and Model B

3.1 Structural Difference

  • Model A: A stable benchmark model with equal fund distribution across assets.
  • Model B: A risk-based model that dynamically adjusts allocation percentages using historical data and risk signals.

3.2 Performance Difference

Expected Annual Return

  • In strong equity years (e.g., S&P 500 return of 28.71% in 2021), Model A may underperform because fixed weights limit upside participation.
  • Model B can identify high-return signals and adjust equity/bond/cash weights more effectively.

Annual Volatility

  • Model A cannot react to market stress due to fixed weights, which may amplify losses (e.g., S&P 500 return of -18.11% in 2022).
  • Model B reduces risk through dynamic weight adjustment under changing market conditions.

Conclusion

Model B provides better downside protection than Model A during volatile periods by incorporating real market indicators, such as the 10-Year Treasury Inflation-Indexed yield and the CBOE Volatility Index (VIX).
